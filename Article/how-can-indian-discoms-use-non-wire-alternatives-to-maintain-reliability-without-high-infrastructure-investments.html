How Can Indian DISCOMs Use Non-Wire Alternatives To Maintain Reliability Without High Infrastructure Investments

The COVID-19 pandemic has presented several challenges to the Indian power system. Most notable amongst these is the depressed electricity demand due to a nationwide lockdown. 

In the recent months, with the lockdown restrictions gradually lifting, India’s maximum demand has also begun to climb steadily, almost reaching the 2019 levels in early-June due to heatwave conditions. On June 15, Delhi recorded a peak power demand of 5805 MW. But at the national level, peak energy demand in May 2020, though 18.5% higher than April 2020, was 13.2% lower than May last year. The average energy demand-per-day in million units for the month was also 15.3% lower than in 2019 (See Figure 1 and Figure 2). In fact, barring the northeast region (NER) grid, the other regions experienced peak energy demand shortages of under 1% (see Table 1).  This could have meant that reliable 24x7 power supply was possible during these months.

Supply reliability at local distribution scale

While the national grid experienced reduced demand and significantly low shortages at the local distribution scale, even when some utilities had excess power, complaints regarding frequent power outages did not stop. Power supply interruptions are usually not captured systematically at a frequency that can be analyzed for each utility. Some utilities do inform consumers in advance of scheduled outages. The Bangalore Electricity Supply Co. Ltd. (BESCOM) for example, has disseminated information on least 10-12 scheduled power outages everyday through its android app for April*. It has fielded several complaints about power supply disruptions on social media, made by irate consumers tackling a newfound challenge of juggling work-from-home schedules with household duties. 

So why have supply disruptions continued? The answer lies in the structure of the distribution network. Our homes receive power from a nearby distribution substation, each with several distribution transformers (DTs), designed to supply a certain predefined level of electric load. This load is usually determined by a distribution company (DISCOM) based on the number of consumers in that area, the category of consumers (domestic, commercial, or industrial), their expected power consumption or connected load, and the typical equipment these consumers use. The distribution lines emanating from the DTs, also called feeders, could therefore be supplying only to residences, commercial buildings or industries, though often they also supply power to a mixed set of loads.

But what happens to the DTs when these loads change? As in the case of the lockdown, with more people staying home, residential energy use has increased. DTs were designed to handle a fixed amount of residential load are now experiencing higher demand from this consumer category. This can lead to transformer overloading and eventually breakdowns. Distribution networks designed and planned for meeting mixed loads also lose balance and the possibility of hotspots in the network increases. 

For DISCOMs to ensure reliable supply, it needs to invest in improvements in the distribution infrastructure by changing or upgrading the DT in addition to other enhancements to prevent supply snags. In our already congested cities, a few hours or days of disruptions, or civil works to lay cables or fix ageing infrastructure as routine maintenance works are expensive activities. Often, these projects take time to be implemented on ground, due to requirement of clearances, finding right contractors and quality equipment to be installed. Capital expenditure on infrastructure improvement projects are therefore difficult for DISCOMs. 

With social distancing measures expected to remain for some time, ensuring that these works continue as planned, with ongoing labor shortages, will be challenging. At such a time, how can DISCOMs avoid expensive and difficult infrastructure enhancements in congested distribution grids while maintaining reliability? 

Non-wire alternatives to the rescue

As the name suggests, non-wire alternatives (NWAs) refer to a package of cost-effective distributed energy resources (DERs) such as energy efficiency, demand response (DR), storage, or distributed renewable energy (DRE) generation that DISCOMs can use as an innovative alternative to traditional transmissions and distribution (T&D) upgrades. NWAs, when planned and designed well, can lead to increased customer engagement and environmental benefits at lower cost compared to the traditional infrastructure development projects. Energy efficiency and DR programs can reduce net demand at peak times in overloaded distribution areas. 

How have utilities in the U.S. used NWAs? 

Lessons on how NWAs can be designed and implemented, largely come from the US where several utilities in different state jurisdictions have undertaken pilot projects that are cost-effective in meeting demand. Examples of NWAs in the US include those addressing an average load reduction between 1 and 85 MW in constrained locations to provide load relief and defer infrastructure investment.

  • In California, the Southern California Edison and Pacific Gas & Electric (PG&E), each implemented multiple NWA projects to address high-load growth areas, planned retirement of nuclear power plants, and events like natural gas storage leaks. 
  • In New York, Con Edison implemented one of the biggest NWAs, Brooklyn Queens Demand Management (BQDM) project, which was estimated to save $1 billion from DERs by deferring investment in a new substation.
  • Another DISCOM in the American mid-west is piloting a two-year NWA program to provide load relief and defer immediate infrastructure investment. The DISCOM is utilizing DSM measures like EE and DR and other DERs in constrained locations to achieve these objectives. 

    • Why do Indian DISCOMs need to consider NWAs? 

    In India, NWAs can be included in the DISCOM’s DSM activities. As of 2017, regulatory commissions in 20 states had notified DSM regulations. The Forum of Regulators (FOR) had developed model DSM regulations in 2010. In 2017, a review conducted by FOR’s working group on DSM noted, that most states have not yet adopted the key features of model DSM regulations. 

    A quick review of Annual Revenue Requirement (ARR) filings of most state-owned DISCOMs shows that reporting on investments made in DSM are incomplete and brief explanation on one-off measures undertaken to promote EE are described in the DSM section of the tariff filing. At the same time, information on capital expenditure for infrastructure additions and improvements feature prominently in ARRs since these justify the possible increases in electricity tariffs.

    For example, for 2019-20, TANGEDCO allocated INR 4,514 crore for distribution related upgrades for the Chennai South region of which INR 1,600 crore – more than 35% – was only for network improvement or meeting out load growth, that is, for “the wires and the poles”. There may be delays in spending allocated capex due to coordination, clearances required, and general time taken in release of funds from different government schemes. The delay in works, interests on capital borrowed, can further aggravate a DISCOM’s financial woes.  TANGEDCO, for example, has been plunged into severe financial stress during the lockdown. 

    Meanwhile, as the load continues to grow, the power infrastructure may continue to fail if non-wire measures are not undertaken. Hence, NWAs are not only needed for maintaining reliability but also to reduce financial losses.  
     

    What preparation is required for NWAs in India?

    Deferring capital-intensive projects through NWAs does need planning and optimization for which DISCOMs need to weigh in its options by analyzing data.  While the concept of NWAs is new in India, it has the potential to decrease overall cost of operations for DISCOMs while providing reliable electricity supply through a cost-effective combination of EE, DR, and DREs in the areas where they are needed the most.  However, there are three key issues that Indian DISCOMs must work through: 

    1. Precise understanding of systems peaks and exact location of bottlenecks: To be able to plan and implement NWAs, DISCOMs need to know the exact locations where additional capacity is needed, when and for how long. Determining locations where there is a need that can be met through NWAs will require an integrated analysis framework to identify its cost-effective configuration. DISCOMs can leverage in-house GIS teams to map load growths and infrastructure constraints. DISCOMs will need to build capacity of their staff in modelling and technical assessments to understand configurations of NWAs that would apply for the location.

    2. Strengthening the regulatory framework: In the US, regulations conducive to consideration of NWAs by utilities have been introduced. In India, through existing DSM regulations, state regulators can mandate utilities to seriously evaluate NWAs, especially EE and DRs to meet demands in congested, distribution-constrained areas. DSM regulations must take a progressive view of NWAs introduced with the view of improving supply reliability and not to impact DISCOM’s electricity sales. Regulators must strongly enforce DSM regulation requirements and encourage DISCOMs to take advantage of technological advancements in better management of loads.

    3. Engage with customers and other stakeholders to create awareness and avoid low-participation risks: The success of the NWAs depends on strong participation from consumers. Awareness and information campaigns can raise participation in efficiency and demand-response programs that reduce peak load. At the same, training and capacity building of utilities is also needed so that they can meaningfully engage with all stakeholders, including their customers, throughout the NWAs design and implementation process.

    NWAs when identified and implemented correctly can lead to quick-wins for DISCOMs committed to improving supply-reliability after the lockdown, without adding to their worsening financial situation.
